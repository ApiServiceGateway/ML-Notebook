Teaching Computers to Guess: Linear Regression Explained

An ultra-simple guide to the math that powers modern AI, explained without the confusing jargon.

AI Researcher
• June 12, 2024 • 10 min read

Connecting the Dots

The simplest way to turn data into predictions.

1. The Intuition: The "Guessing Game"

Imagine you're playing a game. Every time you say a number, I tell you if your guess was too high or too low. Eventually, you'll find the right number. Linear Regression is exactly that.

In machine learning, we try to find a "secret rule" that connects an input (like hours studied) to an output (like test scores). We assume this rule is a straight line:

The Prediction Rule

$$y = m \cdot x + c$$

Output = (How fast it grows) × Input + (The starting point)

How do we know we're wrong?

To learn, the computer needs to know how "bad" its guess was. We calculate the Loss. Think of this like a dartboard: the further your dart (prediction) lands from the bullseye (the real answer), the higher your loss score.

$$Loss = \text{Average of } (\text{Actual} - \text{Predicted})^2$$

We square the difference so that "negative" errors don't cancel out "positive" ones!

2. The Computer's Instructions

Let's look at the actual code. We start with m=0 and c=0 (the computer knows nothing) and let it learn over 15,000 steps. 

import numpy as np

# Creating our simple data: y = 2.5x + 10
features = np.array([5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15])
targets = 2.5 * features + 10

# Starting from scratch
slope_m = 0.0      
intercept_c = 0.0  
step_size = 0.01   
training_steps = 15000

for step in range(training_steps):
    # 1. Make a guess
    predictions = (slope_m * features) + intercept_c
    
    # 2. See how much we missed by
    errors = predictions - targets
    
    # 3. Figure out which direction to move (The Gradient)
    grad_m = 2 * np.mean(errors * features)
    grad_c = 2 * np.mean(errors)
    
    # 4. Take a tiny step in the right direction
    slope_m -= step_size * grad_m
    intercept_c -= step_size * grad_c
    
    if step % 2000 == 0:
        loss = np.mean(errors**2)
        print(f"Step {step}: Loss is {loss:.2f}")

print(f"Result: y = {slope_m:.2f}x + {intercept_c:.2f}")

Line-by-Line Breakdown

  • predictions: This is the computer's current best guess based on its current weight and bias.
  • errors: We subtract the real target from our guess. A positive number means we guessed too high!
  • grad_m & grad_c: This is the magic. These formulas tell us "If you increase m by a tiny bit, will the error go up or down?"
  • step_size: We don't jump straight to the answer because we might miss it. We take tiny, cautious steps.

3. How it Actually Learns

1

Feeling the Ground

Imagine you're on a mountain in a thick fog. You want to reach the valley (lowest loss). You can't see the valley, but you can feel the slope under your feet. This "slope" is the Gradient.

2

Choosing the Speed

The Learning Rate (Step Size) is how big your footsteps are. If they're too big, you might step right over the valley and end up on another mountain! If they're too small, you'll be walking for years.

3

Repetition

The computer repeats this thousands of times. Every single time, it gets a tiny bit closer to the perfect line. Eventually, the "guesses" become indistinguishable from the truth.

Conclusion

Linear Regression isn't magic—it's just a repetitive process of guessing, checking how wrong you are, and adjusting your next guess. By the end of our code's 15,000 steps, the computer discovered that the relationship was exactly y = 2.5x + 10.

Next time you hear about "Deep Learning," remember: it's just thousands of these small linear rules working together in a giant network!
